Working on a 1999 Ford Ranger Its Red

The other day, I had the joy of solo replacing the starter on the ever so cleverly named, dangerranger

•••••••••••

DANGER

•••••••••••

Aptly called that because if you’re laid out underneath one, torquing off the bolts with damn near zero leverage and only 2 (you hope) trusty jack stands holding up 5,000 lbs of redneck engineering, there’s this quiet little voice that pipes up while you’re reefing on a 26-year-old 13mm:

"If this thing shifts just enough, brother, you’re gonna be the meat in a Ford sandwich."


And right then, the truck does that thing. You know the thing. A tiny little creak from the frame, a microscopic wobble in the stand, and suddenly every single one of your pivotal life decisions and moments start playing on the IMAX screen inside your skull at 240 fps.


I had seen myself at 14 years old, learning to drive a Ranger for the first time. Age 19, graduating college, to become a mechanic, to excel at what I was in the act of doing, many years later...


All of it, 4K, Dolby Atmos, the full lifetime timeline, in the two seconds between “pop” and the jack stand settling back into place.


Then the bolt breaks loose, the truck settles, and you exhale a breath you didn’t know you were holding.


You lie there for a second, covered in rust and ATF, heart doing its best impression of a meth’d-up hummingbird, and you laugh. Because you’re still alive, the starter’s finally loose, and somewhere in the cosmos the universe just shrugged and said, “Eh, not today, buddy.”


That, my friends, is the Danger Ranger experience. All in the name of keeping a good thing going.
